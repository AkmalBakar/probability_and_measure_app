# Level 20: Markov Chains

## Reading

The infection process and load balancing processes share a special feature: the state at step $n$ depends only on the state at step $n - 1$, and not on the value of $n$ or the history of the process. This is the **Markov property**, and processes with this property are called Markov chains.

### Definition and transition matrices

**Definition 3 (Markov process, Markov kernel, Markov chain, transition matrix).** Let $(E, \Sigma)$ be a measurable space, and let $\kappa : \Sigma \times E \to [0, 1]$ be a function. Suppose that for each $S \in \Sigma$ the function $x \to \kappa(S, x)$ is $\Sigma$-measurable on $E$, and for each fixed $x_0 \in E$ the function $S \to \kappa(S, x_0)$ defines a probability measure on $(E, \Sigma)$. Then we say $\kappa$ is a **Markov kernel**.

Suppose $\mu_0, \mu_1, \ldots$ is a sequence of probability measures on $(E, \Sigma)$, and suppose that for each $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $A \in \Sigma$ we have

$$\mu_n(Y) = \int_Y \kappa(Y, x) \, \mathrm{d}\mu_{n-1}(x).$$

Then we say $(\mu_n)$ is a **Markov process** generated by $\kappa$, with initial distribution $\mu_0$.

If $E$ is finite, we can replace $\kappa$ with a **transition matrix** $A$, whose rows and columns are indexed by $E$, whose entries are in $[0, 1]$, and which satisfies: for each $x_0 \in E$, $\sum_{y \in E} A_{yx_0} = 1$. Then

$$\mu_n(y) = \sum_{j \in E} A_{yj} \mu_{n-1}(j),$$

and $(\mu_n)$ is a **Markov chain**. The key formula is $\mu_n = A^n \mu_0$.

### Transition digraphs and communication

The **transition digraph** of $A$ is a directed graph on $E$, with an arc $\overrightarrow{ji}$ whenever $A_{ij} > 0$ (the weight of the arc is $A_{ji}$). State $j$ is **reachable** from $i$ if there is a directed path from $i$ to $j$. If $i$ is reachable from $j$ and vice versa, we say $i$ and $j$ **communicate** — this is an equivalence relation. If $E$ is the unique communicating class, the chain is **irreducible**.

A set $S$ of states is **absorbing** if there is no arc leaving $S$. An absorbing set that is also a communicating class is a **terminal set**.

A **closed walk** of length $k$ is a sequence of not necessarily distinct vertices with arcs between consecutive ones and from last to first. The **periodicity** of $x$ is the GCD of the lengths of all closed walks containing $x$.

### Spectral theory of transition matrices

**Theorem 4.** Let $A$ be the transition matrix of a Markov chain. Then:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ have absolute value at most 1, and 1 is an eigenvalue of $A$. If $A$ is symmetric, all eigenvalues are real.
2. If $s$ is not in any terminal set, then for all $t$: $\lim_{n \to \infty} A^n_{st} = 0$.
3. The geometric multiplicity of eigenvalue 1 equals the number of terminal sets, and a non-zero eigenvector with eigenvalue 1 is strictly positive (or strictly negative) on at least one terminal set.
4. States in the same communicating class have the same periodicity.
5. For each $k \geq 2$, each terminal set of periodicity $k$ contributes 1 to the geometric multiplicity of $e^{2\pi i \ell / k}$ for each $1 \leq \ell \leq k$; there are no other contributions.

*Key proof idea:* Eigenvalues have $|\lambda| \leq 1$ because if $\mathbf{e}$ is an eigenvector with $\lambda \mathbf{e}_j = (A^T \mathbf{e})_j = \sum_i A_{ji} \mathbf{e}_i$, taking $j$ to maximize $|\mathbf{e}_j|$: $|\lambda| |\mathbf{e}_j| \leq \sum_i A_{ij} |\mathbf{e}_i| \leq |\mathbf{e}_j| \sum_i A_{ij} = |\mathbf{e}_j|$.

### Stationary distributions

A **stationary distribution** of $A$ is a probability distribution $\mu$ such that $A\mu = \mu$. Every transition matrix has at least one stationary distribution. It has exactly one stationary distribution if and only if there is exactly one terminal set.

**Definition 5 (Ergodic).** If $A$ is irreducible and has periodicity 1, we say $A$ is **ergodic**.

An ergodic chain has 1 as an eigenvalue with multiplicity 1, and all other eigenvalues have absolute value strictly less than 1.

### Convergence theorem

**Theorem 6.** Let $A$ be an $n$ by $n$ ergodic matrix with stationary distribution $\pi$. Let $\lambda$ be the largest absolute value of any eigenvalue other than 1. Then for any $\mu_0$, letting $\mu_t = A^t \mu_0$:

$$\|\mu_t - \pi\|_\infty \leq t^n \lambda^{t-n}.$$

This gives exponentially fast convergence to the stationary distribution, though the bound is crude. The convergence rate depends on the **spectral gap** $1 - \lambda$.

### Lazy random walks on graphs

A useful construction: given an undirected graph $G$ on $n$ vertices, the **lazy random walk** has transition matrix

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2} & \text{if } i = j \\ \frac{1}{2d(i)} & \text{if } ij \text{ is an edge} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

where $d(i)$ is the degree of vertex $i$. The "lazy" part (staying put with probability $\frac{1}{2}$) ensures periodicity 1, making the chain ergodic when $G$ is connected. The stationary distribution is $\pi_i = \frac{d(i)}{2|E(G)|}$.

## Key Results

### definition: Markov chain and transition matrix
**Number:** Definition 3
**Plain English:** A Markov chain is a random process where the next state depends only on the current state (not on history). For finite state spaces, it is fully described by a transition matrix $A$ with $\mu_n = A^n \mu_0$.
**Formal:** $(E, \Sigma)$ measurable space, $\kappa$ Markov kernel, $\mu_n(Y) = \int_Y \kappa(Y,x) \, \mathrm{d}\mu_{n-1}(x)$. For finite $E$: $A$ is a matrix with non-negative entries and column sums 1, $\mu_n = A\mu_{n-1}$.
**Load-bearing:** yes

### theorem: Spectral theory of Markov chains
**Number:** Theorem 4
**Plain English:** The eigenvalues of a transition matrix all have absolute value $\leq 1$. The number of eigenvalues equal to 1 counts the terminal sets. States not in terminal sets lose all probability mass as $n \to \infty$.
**Formal:** See five-part statement above.
**Proof sketch:** $|\lambda| \leq 1$ by taking the entry of maximum absolute value in the eigenvector. Multiplicity of 1 equals number of terminal sets by Perron-Frobenius and the block structure. Periodicity parts use closed walk arguments.
**Key technique:** Linear algebra, Perron-Frobenius theorem
**Depends on:** Linear algebra
**Used by:** Theorem 6 (convergence), stationary distributions
**Load-bearing:** yes

### definition: Ergodic Markov chain
**Number:** Definition 5
**Plain English:** An ergodic chain is irreducible (one communicating class) and aperiodic (periodicity 1). It has a unique stationary distribution and converges to it from any start.
**Formal:** $A$ is ergodic if it is irreducible and has periodicity 1.
**Load-bearing:** yes

### theorem: Convergence to stationary distribution
**Number:** Theorem 6
**Plain English:** An ergodic Markov chain converges exponentially fast to its unique stationary distribution. The rate is controlled by the second-largest eigenvalue.
**Formal:** For $A$ ergodic with stationary distribution $\pi$ and spectral gap parameter $\lambda$: $\|\mu_t - \pi\|_\infty \leq t^n \lambda^{t-n}$.
**Proof sketch:** Not proved in detail (uses spectral decomposition $A^t = \sum \lambda_i^t \mathbf{v}_i \mathbf{v}_i^T$).
**Key technique:** Spectral decomposition, eigenvalue bounds
**Depends on:** Theorem 4
**Used by:** Mixing time analysis, MCMC
**Load-bearing:** yes

## Exercises

### exercise: Transition matrix preserves probability measures
**Number:** Exercise 4
**Difficulty:** warm-up
**Tags:** Markov-chain, transition-matrix, probability-measure

**Question:**
Prove that if $A$ is a transition matrix and $\mu$ is any probability measure on $(E, \mathcal{P}(E))$, then $\mu' = A\mu$ (defined by $\mu'(S) = \sum_{y \in S} \sum_{j \in E} A_{yj} \mu(j)$) is also a probability measure on $(E, \mathcal{P}(E))$.

**Hint 1:** Check non-negativity and $\mu'(\emptyset) = 0$
Non-negativity: $A_{yj} \geq 0$ and $\mu(j) \geq 0$, so the sum is non-negative. $\mu'(\emptyset) = 0$: empty sum evaluates to 0.

**Hint 2:** Check $\mu'(E) = 1$
$\mu'(E) = \sum_{y \in E} \sum_{j \in E} A_{yj}\mu(j) = \sum_{j \in E} \mu(j) \sum_{y \in E} A_{yj} = \sum_{j \in E} \mu(j) \cdot 1 = 1$, using the column-sum property of $A$.

**Solution:**
$\mu'$ takes non-negative values because the entries of $A$ are non-negative and $\mu$ is non-negative. Also $\mu'(\emptyset) = 0$ since it is an empty sum. For finite additivity: since $E$ is finite, this is vacuously countable additivity. Finally:

$$\mu'(E) = \sum_{y \in E} \sum_{j \in E} A_{yj}\mu(j) = \sum_{j \in E} \mu(j) \sum_{y \in E} A_{yj} = \sum_{j \in E} \mu(j) \cdot 1 = 1$$

where we swapped finite sums and used $\sum_{y \in E} A_{yj} = 1$ (column sums equal 1). $\square$

### exercise: Stationary distribution of infection model
**Number:** Exercise 5
**Difficulty:** standard
**Tags:** Markov-chain, stationary-distribution, infection-model, convergence

**Question:**
Determine the stationary distribution $\pi$ for the infection process of Example 17 (Notes 4). How does this compare to the stationary point of the associated ODE as $\rho$ and $\lambda$ vary?

Suppose $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\rho = \frac{1}{4}$, and $Z_0 = 0.75N$. Determine a lower bound on the smallest number of steps $m$ such that $\|\mu_m - \pi\|_\infty < 0.1$.

By considering a sequence of steps leading to the stationary distribution, determine an upper bound on $m$. *(Hint: What is the simplest way this Markov chain might reach the stationary distribution?)*

**Hint 1:** Find the terminal set
The state space is $\{0, 1, \ldots, N\}$. From any state $n$, there is an arc from $n$ to $n-1$ (recovery), and (if $n < N$) from $n$ to $n+1$ (infection). Since there is an arc from 1 to 0, and the only arc at 0 is a self-loop, $\{0\}$ is the unique terminal set. So $\pi = \delta_0$ (all mass at 0).

**Hint 2:** For the lower bound, use the ODE analysis
When $\lambda > \rho$ (i.e. $\frac{1}{2} > \frac{1}{4}$), the process stays near $0.5N$ for exponentially long (Exercise 12 from Notes 4). So $\|\mu_m - \pi\|_\infty \geq 0.99$ for $m \leq ce^N$ for some constant $c$. For the upper bound, consider successive transitions $n \to n-1 \to \cdots \to 0$.

**Solution:**
For every $1 \leq n \leq N$, there is an arc from $n$ to $n-1$ and (if $n < N$) from $n$ to $n+1$ in the transition digraph, so $\{1, 2, \ldots, N\}$ forms a communicating class. Since there is an arc from 1 to 0, and the only arc at 0 is a probability 1 loop, $\{0\}$ is the unique terminal set and the stationary distribution has probability 1 at state 0.

When $\lambda > \rho$, this is **not** the ODE stationary point (which is $\frac{\lambda - \rho}{\lambda}N$). The process will eventually reach 0 (the disease dies out), but it takes exponentially long in $N$.

**Lower bound:** By Exercise 12 of Notes 4, with $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}$, $\rho = \frac{1}{4}$, starting at $Z_0 = 0.75N$, the process stays in $[0.1N, 0.9N]$ with probability at least 0.99 for time $T^* = 0.04N \exp(Ne^{-100}/500)$. At time $T^*$, $\|\mu_{T^*} - \pi\|_\infty \geq 0.99$ since the mass is concentrated far from 0.

**Upper bound:** The easiest route to 0 is a sequence of "downwards" transitions: no new infections occur and every step a person recovers. The probability of one such step is at least $(1 - \lambda) \cdot \frac{1}{N}$ (no infection, and we pick an infected person to recover). Starting at $N$ infected, the probability of reaching 0 in $N$ steps is at least $((1 - \lambda)/N)^N$. So after $k = 20N^{N+1}(1-\lambda)^{-N}$ steps (in blocks of $N$), the probability of not having reached 0 is at most $(1 - ((1-\lambda)/N)^N)^{k/N} \leq e^{-10} < 0.1$. This gives an upper bound of $m = 20N^{N+1}(1-\lambda)^{-N}$.

### exercise: Lazy random walk
**Number:** Exercise 6
**Difficulty:** standard
**Tags:** Markov-chain, random-walk, graph, stationary-distribution

**Question:**
For a connected graph $G$ on $n$ vertices with $m$ edges, verify that the lazy random walk has stationary distribution $\pi_i = \frac{d(i)}{2m}$, and that the chain is ergodic.

**Hint 1:** Check $A\pi = \pi$
Compute $(A\pi)_i = \frac{1}{2}\pi_i + \sum_{j: ij \in E} \frac{1}{2d(j)} \cdot \frac{d(j)}{2m} = \frac{1}{2}\pi_i + \frac{d(i)}{4m}$...

**Hint 2:** Ergodicity
Irreducibility follows from $G$ being connected. Periodicity 1 follows from the self-loops ($A_{ii} = \frac{1}{2} > 0$).

**Solution:**
**Stationary distribution:** We need to check $A\pi = \pi$, i.e., $(A\pi)_i = \pi_i$ for all $i$:

$$(A\pi)_i = \frac{1}{2}\pi_i + \sum_{j \sim i} \frac{1}{2d(j)} \pi_j = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{d(i)}{2m} + \sum_{j \sim i} \frac{1}{2d(j)} \cdot \frac{d(j)}{2m} = \frac{d(i)}{4m} + \frac{d(i)}{4m} = \frac{d(i)}{2m} = \pi_i.$$

**Ergodicity:** Since $G$ is connected, for any two vertices $i, j$ there is a path from $i$ to $j$ in the transition digraph (using the edges of $G$). So the chain is irreducible. Since $A_{ii} = \frac{1}{2} > 0$ for all $i$, there is a closed walk of length 1 at every state, so the periodicity is 1. Hence the chain is ergodic. $\square$
